BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

There are barriers to politics for young people.

The emerging generation has had more chances than ever before to engage with how Governments are run, through the fluidity and freedom of social-media driven technology and the pace of modern day communication. It's interesting to see that despite this accessibility, there is still a large portion of young people who have never voted or make ill informed decisions and waste their vote.

Our strategy to approach this problem revolves around the concept of interactivity. The intention of our solution is to enable people to allot value to certain areas of concern, while being cleverly immersed within a story or gameplay narrative.

Such an environment promotes a much more intuitive and fun experience than traditional forced-choice questions. By projecting themselves into the experience, the project remains non-partisan and enables the user to project their values to see how they compare with the political parties.

THE BRIEF

Develop an interactive web based tool to encourage, inform and educate voters in the upcoming general election.

Our work began as part of a 300 level Massey University Design & Business paper, where small teams were allocated to create a high quality design solution to cater for a specific client.

 

 

OUR CONCEPT

On the Fence is a metaphor for being undecided. Sheep have a distinct relevance to kiwi culture.

In principle, there is a lack of interactivity in Politics, especially at election time. It is still broadcast-driven, only once every three years are constituents able to have their say; it is plain to see that a trend is emerging. Politics is uninteresting and there is a group mentality, lack of individuality and personal responsibility is especially prevalent amongst first time voters, in the late teen/early 20's age group. Younger people in our peer group don't see voting as enjoyable and it has no shaping influence in their lives.

On the Fence is an opportunity to put 'trainer wheels' on the future for them. We aim to encourage the user to take control and search for knowledge themselves, by pushing the idea of a game we want to engage people so that they can discover, learn and absorb a more political world view.

 

THE INSPIRATION


Our Left/Right statements have been inspired by the Left vs Right concept map.

Written and art-directed by David McCandless in 2011, they act as a starting point in order to focus our own statements on relevant policy areas.

 

THE STATEMENTS

Put trainer wheels on the future.

After analysing the overall structure and process that was needed in order to get the user's political recommendation back to them, we soon determined that using a factual or question-orientated approach to filtering out their preferences was inadequate. A forced-choice question based approach is in many ways a soft interrogation and that makes the overall experience less fun and less engaging. An important consideration was putting the statements in a language that a political novice may begin to understand - not by dumbing down their significance, but by explaining their fundamentals in as simple a manner as possible. They could not be too long because of typographic restraints, but more importantly: the user may become confused.

Our process of determining value for each of the policy areas to compare user data to starts with the web application we have built to gather back-end data. It works in much of the same way a statement may be polled by the media or by the Electoral Commission. Rather than using a binary yes/no system, the sliders enable the user to position themselves relatively to each statement. Rather than agree or disagree with one, they could be shades of either. In order to keep the average values as true as possible, we are have sought feedback from a varied consensus of academics, scholars and professionals. These include Politics and Humanities lecturers and researchers from Victoria University, Senior Tutors and Lecturers from Massey University, Educational Psychologists, Research Laboratory Scientists, leaders from young political parties such as Young Nat's & Young Labour and more. In the process of consultation, the statements have been refined to the ones currently used in the application.

 
 

OUR SPECIALIST PANEL

We recognized the need to validate where parties stand on
each of these statements and to do so from a broad, but
balanced range of political views.

In order to be transparent and remain non-partisan, our process and methodology needs verification and feedback. The process begins with requesting input from a wide range of people with knowledge or expertise in the political arena that can give an informed opinion. Their input is necessary to determine the numeric values that will help the application calculate the user averages against.

The system that we have developed places control in the user's hands. By creating a questionnaire for both the user and the panel, the traditional forced-choice user centric approach to polling is reversed and the focus is put on comparing what the user thinks they believe, against what the panel has determined. The goal is to find common ground between the parties and the users values without reference to policies. The panel acts as a mediator.

 

THE PARTIES

The political parties represented in On the Fence are the current top 7 polling parties, with seats that represent New Zealanders in 49th Parliament.

All Parties were contacted through their General Party Secretaries, and were briefed about the project. In order to remain completely transparent and non-partisan, all data and information was readily and speedily available. Permission was sought to include them in the application. Official expressed endorsement was received by the Green Party, United Future, Mana, Labour, Māori Party and National.

The Electoral Commission was a part of the consultation process throughout the project.

CALCULATING RESULTS

Using the averages on all parties gained from our panel, we compare these to the user to see how close they match.

We use a simple percentage comparison technique, where one value placed by a user is directly compared to what value the party was placed at.

This method allows us to also take into account situations where having more of a priority over a value than a party, inherently makes you less compatible. (Similarily having less points towards a value will also cause the percentage to drop).

The slider system freely allows for the user to agree, or disagree with both statements. By agreeing or disagreeing with both, the User is, in effect, agreeing or disagreeing with neither; the value remains at 50. The degree to which one is favoured more over the other becomes the users' final position in relation to that policy area. It takes into consideration the balancing act needed when comparing two contrasting left/right views, and finding shades of both.
